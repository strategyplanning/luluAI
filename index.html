The Movement Nation Constraint
AI savings don't just fund the Movement Nation — the Movement Nation determines which savings are permissible.

A scenario that guts Educators to fund embassies has destroyed the human layer that makes embassies work. The people are the strategy. This tool enforces that constraint: back-office and operational efficiency fund the vision; frontline community roles are sacred infrastructure, protected by design. Where retail roles are optimized, they are redeployed into Movement Nation programming — not eliminated.

Slider Guide

The three questions, their options, and the rationale for each

Each slider represents a distinct decision or belief. This guide explains what each setting implies and when it makes sense.

1. How fast do we believe AI capabilities will improve?

This is a belief about the world, not a strategic choice. It determines the ceiling on what AI can automate within the planning horizon.
Slow
Today's tools plateau. LLMs and automation hit diminishing returns within 6–12 months. Plan around current-generation capabilities only. Conservative assumption — appropriate if you believe AI progress is overhyped or regulatory constraints will slow deployment.
Moderate
Steady, compounding improvement each quarter. New capabilities unlock new use cases at a predictable pace. The consensus view among most enterprise AI teams. Plans built on this assumption have the widest margin of safety.
Rapid
Step-change capabilities within 12–18 months. Agentic systems handle complex workflows end-to-end. Roles that seem safe today become automatable. Appropriate if you believe frontier labs are about to deliver a capability leap — and that lululemon should plan for it now.

2. How fast can we integrate and scale AI capabilities?

This is a strategic and operational choice. Even if AI improves rapidly, integration speed depends on internal readiness, change management, and risk appetite.
Conservative
Deploy only proven, low-risk AI in back-office functions. Pilot before scaling. Prioritize employee experience and change management. Slower capital unlock but minimal execution risk. Appropriate if the organisation has limited AI maturity or leadership bandwidth.
Moderate
Expand AI into merchandising, supply chain, and GEC automation in parallel. Dedicated transformation team drives adoption. Balanced pace — meaningful savings within 12–18 months without overwhelming the organisation. The most common posture for large retailers.
Aggressive
Maximum AI deployment across every non-sacred function simultaneously. Requires significant upfront investment in tooling, talent, and change management. Largest capital unlock but highest execution risk. Appropriate if competitive pressure or market conditions demand urgency.

3. How much freed capital should fund Movement Nation?

This is the core strategic allocation question. It determines how AI savings are split between funding the vision and expanding margins.
0% — All to margin
Every dollar of AI savings flows to the bottom line. Maximum near-term margin expansion and earnings recovery. Appropriate if the board believes the current strategy is sufficiently funded and the priority is restoring investor confidence through profitability.
50/50 split
Half funds Movement Nation, half flows to margin. Demonstrates fiscal discipline to investors while visibly investing in the long-term moat. The balanced posture — appropriate when you need to serve both the stock price and the 10-year brand thesis simultaneously.
100% — All to MN
Every dollar of AI savings is reinvested into Movement Nation: flagship embassies, ambassador ecosystems, citizenship platforms, community programming. No near-term margin benefit. Appropriate if the board has conviction that Movement Nation is the primary value driver and is willing to defer earnings expansion.
